Memory biases and

Dear Colleague,
I am delighted to invite you to Cambridge Assessment Network's autumn seminar: Memory biases and curricular illusions with Dr Helen Abadzi, which we will be hosting on Thursday 20 October at Downing College, Cambridge.
 
How is learning limited by the brain’s working memory?
As humans, we are only partly aware of how we learn. Memory limitations create systematic biases when people make decisions or predict how others learn. Such biases may significantly affect the decisions of curriculum developers, textbook authors, and policymakers.
Dr Abadzi, of the University of Texas, USA, will be drawing on links between education theory, empirical work on cognition, memory and brain physiology to explore the implications for teaching and learning.
Recent discussion in England about the link between educational theory and empirical work on cognition, memory and brain physiology has been mired in controversy and inconclusive disputes between different fields and camps. Yet there remains an urgent need to reconcile the tensions and ensure that curriculum and assessment theory does not ignore important insights from cognitive science.
This free event is part of the Cambridge Festival of Ideas 2016, so we suggest you book soon to be sure of a place. There will be a Q&A session with Dr Abadzi at the end of her seminar.

About our speaker
Dr Helen Abadzi is a Greek psychologist and polyglot, who spent 27 years as a senior education specialist at the World Bank. To improve the outcomes of education projects she has resorted to cognitive psychology and neuroscience.
She monitors regularly the emerging research in these fields and synthesises relevant findings to explain and predict likely outcomes from various interventions. Her work raised early-grade reading fluency into an international priority. She is interested in studying the building blocks that lead to automatic execution of complex skills, such as maths, reading, and rapid comprehension of Arabic.
